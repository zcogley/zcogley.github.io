Teaching

At the core of my pedagogical strategy is a commitment to teaching students the skills of analytical thinking and writing through extensive classroom participation and modeling of effective philosophical argumentation. I begin every new course with the explanation that significant reading and participation will be demanded, and I pass out a handout with introductory phrases like “I agree with ____ because,” “I have a different opinion,” and “Can you explain that in more detail?” I explain that we’re going to have conversations in class and so students must preface their comments with these kinds of introductory phrases. This ensures that students are actually listening to each other’s contributions, rather than simply waiting for their chance to talk. I further enhance the amount and depth of participation by structuring my courses around an “on-call” method of class participation where I direct questions to previously selected students. This method develops their ability to present arguments clearly and carefully in a less stressful situation than being cold-called.

Before every class meeting, I require students to respond to the readings by submitting posts to the course website that seek clarification about or challenge a claim in the assigned readings. This enables me to tailor discussion of the material to student difficulties and interests. I spend class time walking students through how to write an analytical paper and show them how to think of objections and replies that need to be considered. This means students are able to do original thinking and provide argumentation that does not just parrot the material they have read. Further, I always require students to write rough drafts and participate in peer review before submitting their final drafts. Students are more motivated when their peers will see their work, and the chance to revise with peer feedback always makes their work better. I emphasize that revising is more than just making editorial changes. Real revising can require tearing a paper apart to get at its central argument and then rewriting the paper around a new line of reasoning. By the end of the term, students make significant progress in writing a clear and forcefully argued philosophy paper.

To further assist in digesting the difficult source material and to supplement our weekly class meetings, I offer video webcasts of all course meetings for students to review. If students fail to grasp a point in class, they can review the class meeting later. The webcasts are also beneficial because students who have to miss class can easily review what they missed. I find that questions in office hours are more focused on a deeper understanding of the material when students review the lectures before we meet. Finally, using the video feeds helps me to improve my pedagogy by giving me a student’s view of my classroom.

By structuring assignments and classroom interaction in these ways, I aim to create an environment where I inculcate in students an appreciation for and an interest in philosophy, help them achieve clarity in their thoughts and writing, facilitate students’ understanding of the arguments relevant to an issue, and model respect for others and their ideas.
